# Lessons Learned

## 1. Shift-left cost visibility is more powerful than month-end reporting

Monthly reports are useful, but they are late. By the time the bill is reviewed, the infrastructure change has already been deployed and may already be supporting production traffic.

Putting cost visibility into Pull Requests changed the timing of the conversation. Teams could challenge more expensive changes before they became part of the baseline.

## 2. FinOps adoption is mostly cultural

The technical implementation was important, but the largest change was behavioral. Engineers had to start treating cost as part of architecture quality.

## 3. PR comments work because they are close to the decision

A dashboard can be ignored. A PR comment appears exactly where reviewers are already deciding whether to approve or request changes.

## 4. Not every cost increase is bad

Some cost increases are justified by growth, reliability, performance or compliance. The goal of the project was not to block all increases.

## 5. Commitment optimization requires continuous review

Savings Plans and Reserved Instances are not a one-time activity. Coverage can drift as workloads change, usage grows or commitments expire.

## 6. Tagging is governance infrastructure

Tags are the foundation for cost attribution, dashboards, ownership and optimization decisions.

## 7. Executive reporting needs a simple story

Leadership needs to understand what changed, what it cost before, what it costs now, what savings were achieved, what risks remain and what actions are next.
